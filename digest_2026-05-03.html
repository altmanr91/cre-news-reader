Housing lotteries frustrate landlords, tenants. Here’s how to fix them

The Real Deal — New York — Sat, 02 May 2026 13:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

This opinion piece argues that New York City's housing lottery system, designed to fill income-restricted apartments, is inefficient and creates unnecessary vacancies, harming both tenants and affordable housing operators. The author highlights that below-market units typically remain vacant for four to five months due to bureaucratic processes, overwhelming application volumes, and mismatches between applicants and available units. Drawing on recommendations from the NYU Furman Center, the article suggests reforms such as pre-screening applicants for eligibility, gathering more detailed preferences to avoid unwanted offers, and creating a unified document submission system across various government housing programs to streamline the process.

Japanese capital floods NYC multifamily market

The Real Deal — New York — Sat, 02 May 2026 13:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

Japanese investors have become a dominant force in New York City's real estate market, acquiring over $2.1 billion in property since early 2024. While historically focused on trophy assets, a significant portion of this capital is now targeting smaller multifamily walk-ups, injecting much-needed liquidity into a sluggish investment sales market. This shift is driven by higher yields in the U.S. compared to Japan's low interest rates, the ability for Japanese investors to secure cheaper domestic financing, and a unique tax code allowing accelerated depreciation for older wood-frame buildings. This makes New York's aging multifamily stock particularly attractive, especially for properties where building value exceeds land value.

The Upper East Side’s court-ordered market

The Real Deal — New York — Sat, 02 May 2026 12:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

This article highlights a trend of court-ordered sales in the Upper East Side townhouse market, often resulting in significant discounts due to financial distress and legal disputes. Several high-profile cases are detailed, including a recent $10 million sale of an East 73rd Street home following a hedge funder's loan default, and the $34.5 million sale of a townhouse formerly owned by Oleg Cassini's sister-in-law, which involved a Chapter 11 filing and a "tortured" sales process. These situations underscore how legal entanglements and the high carrying costs of luxury properties can force sellers to accept lower prices, especially when facing foreclosure or bankruptcy.

Chicago brokerage leaders weigh in on MLS’s national expansion

The Real Deal — Chicago — Sat, 02 May 2026 14:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

Chicago brokerage leaders are evaluating the national expansion of Midwest Real Estate Data (MRED), the local Multiple Listing Service, which now offers access to any licensed agent nationwide, including its unique Private Listing Network. This move, supported by Compass International Holdings, aims to provide greater choice for home sellers and increased transparency for buyers by allowing agents to list properties that are not publicly visible but accessible to all MLS agents. While some see this as a positive step towards a national MLS and broader sharing of private inventory, others are uncertain about its impact on local Chicago agents and consumer behavior, emphasizing the need for agents to adapt to evolving listing strategies.

The Daily Dirt: Is AI the office market’s best friend or biggest threat?

The Real Deal — New York — Sat, 02 May 2026 11:30:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

This article explores the dual impact of artificial intelligence on the commercial real estate market, noting that while AI companies are currently driving significant office leasing activity in major tech hubs like New York and San Francisco, the technology itself poses a long-term threat to office demand. Brokerages like CBRE, Newmark, and JLL reported strong first-quarter earnings, largely attributed to AI firms securing large office spaces, often exceeding their immediate headcount needs. However, the increasing efficiency and potential for automation offered by AI could lead to reduced office space requirements for companies in the future, a trend already hinted at by tech layoffs tied to AI investments. The article also highlights the intense demand for data centers, with vacancy rates nearing zero due to AI-driven needs.
