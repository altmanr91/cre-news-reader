The Wave is Here: What Multifamily Loan Maturities Mean for Owners, Lenders & Brokers in 2026

REBusinessOnline — Fri, 12 Jun 2026 16:02:16 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

This article analyzes the impending wave of multifamily loan maturities in 2026, highlighting the challenges owners face due to elevated interest rates and tightened underwriting standards. Many owners who secured low-interest loans in 2020-2021 are now over-leveraged, with increased operating expenses, slowed rent growth, and declining valuations making refinancing difficult. This situation is expected to drive a surge in transaction activity, including forced sales, as well-capitalized sponsors re-enter the market to acquire assets that are no longer viable for their current owners. The author advises owners to be proactive, realistic, and plan early for refinancing, selling, or bringing in capital partners.

DATA POINTS

Multifamily mortgages maturing in 2026: 13%

March Consumer Price Index (CPI): 3.3% over prior 12 months

Federal Reserve benchmark funds rate (late April): 3.5% to 3.75%

MARKET INTELLIGENCE

The Mortgage Bankers Association's 2025 Commercial Real Estate Survey of Loan Maturity Volumes indicates that 13% of multifamily mortgages will mature in 2026. Interest rates reached record lows in 2020-2021, but the Federal Reserve has since raised rates in response to inflation, with the benchmark funds rate between 3.5% and 3.75% in late April. Operating expenses have increased, rent growth has slowed, cap rates have widened, and valuations have declined, leading to tighter underwriting and smaller refinance loan amounts.

Pocket listings face another setback as New York advances crackdown

The Real Deal — New York — Fri, 12 Jun 2026 23:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

CRE Legal / Regulatory

New York's state Legislature has passed the Fair and Transparent Real Estate Listings Act, a bill aimed at increasing transparency in property marketing by requiring agents to publicly market properties unless sellers or landlords explicitly opt out. This legislation, which mandates public listing on widely accessible platforms, is a response to evolving practices in private listings and seeks to ensure broader exposure and competition for properties. If signed into law by Governor Kathy Hochul, it could serve as a model for other states, following similar measures adopted in Washington, Wisconsin, and Connecticut, with Illinois and Hawaii also considering such regulations. Proponents argue that greater transparency benefits consumers by potentially leading to higher sale prices and more offers, while also deterring agents from exclusively pushing private listing networks.

“Shock to the luxury real estate market”: Pied-à-terre tax rollout poses challenges

The Real Deal — New York — Fri, 12 Jun 2026 16:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

New York City's new pied-à-terre tax, set to take effect in July, targets second homes valued over $5 million, including co-op and condo units not used as primary residences. While projected to generate up to $1 billion in revenue, the tax faces significant implementation challenges, particularly concerning residency status verification, property valuation for co-ops, and compliance. Experts anticipate potential litigation and disputes due to ambiguities in the proposed rules, which heavily emphasize penalties for non-compliance rather than clear guidance for homeowners and co-op boards. The retroactive application to January 1, 2026, and the short timeframe for homeowners to prove primary residency status are expected to create substantial hurdles for affected property owners.
