CBRE’s Scott Schnuckel On Why Retail Is Back in Favor

Commercial Observer — Wed, 20 May 2026 18:45:28 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

CBRE's managing director of retail for the Americas, Scott Schnuckel, discusses the resurgence of the retail real estate sector, attributing its strength to record-low vacancy rates and minimal new construction. He notes that the landlord community is benefiting from a long-term trend of limited new shopping center development, coupled with the demolition of older malls and a growth in retail brands, particularly value-oriented mass merchants, grocers, off-price retailers, and food and beverage concepts. While rent growth has been modest due to long-term lease contracts, investor demand is increasing, with retail becoming a favored sector for large funds seeking stability, especially for grocery-anchored and well-executed mixed-use projects. Schnuckel highlights that new construction is primarily occurring in high-growth Southern and West Coast markets like Phoenix, Texas, and Florida, where population expansion supports the development of new suburbs and incremental business for retailers.

Build-to-Rent Investors Catch a Break in U.S. House Bill

SUMMARY

Infrastructure Investment / Government Policy

The U.S. House of Representatives has approved a revised bipartisan housing affordability bill, the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act, which removes a contentious provision from the Senate version that would have mandated institutional investors sell build-to-rent (BTR) single-family homes to individual buyers after seven years. PolicyPro: J-51 reboot flops for rent-regulated landlords, boosts co-ops and condos

The Real Deal — New York — Wed, 20 May 2026 22:30:00 +0000

SUMMARY

CRE Legal / Regulatory

The New York State Legislature is poised to approve a reboot of the J-51 property tax abatement program, extending it through 2036 and broadening eligibility for co-ops and condos. While the revamped program increases the benefit cap to 100% of reasonable rehabilitation costs and mandates biennial updates to cost schedules, it maintains the 50% affordability requirement for rental buildings, effectively excluding many rent-stabilized properties. This outcome is seen as a significant disappointment for owners of rent-regulated buildings, who sought a broader overhaul to include properties with at least 90% rent-regulated units. Conversely, co-ops and condos will benefit from a raised assessed value threshold for eligibility, which will also be indexed to inflation, and a cap on abatements at 50% of annual property tax, a limit not expected to deter participation.
