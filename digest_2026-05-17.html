No profit, no project: Reynoso tries to demystify development

The Real Deal — New York — Sat, 16 May 2026 13:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

This article discusses Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso's conditional recommendation for the Monitor Point development, a proposed 1,150-unit project on the Greenpoint waterfront. Reynoso's recommendation uniquely includes specific financial details, such as the 6.25% to 6.5% yield required by financiers for initial capital lending, to illustrate the economic realities of housing development. He emphasizes that developers need to generate profit to secure funding, and that public land projects like Monitor Point, which require upfront and ongoing costs to the developer, differ from those where land is transferred at nominal cost. Despite this detailed financial explanation, Reynoso ultimately aligns with the local Council member's demand for 50% affordable units, suggesting a mix of moderate-income households to make the project financially viable and socially diverse.

“It’s going to hurt”: NYC resi players push back on proposed cash sales tax

The Real Deal — New York — Sat, 16 May 2026 12:39:11 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

New York state lawmakers are considering a new 1% tax on residential cash sales of $1 million or more in New York City, a proposal projected to generate $160 million annually to help fund the city's budget deficit. This potential tax, along with a recently announced annual levy on second homes valued at $5 million or more, has drawn significant criticism from real estate professionals. Industry experts express concern that the lack of clear details and the cumulative effect of these taxes could deter transactions, particularly given that cash deals accounted for 65% of all sales in the city last year, rising to 75% for sales over $1 million. Critics also suggest the projected revenue may be overstated, as it doesn't account for potential changes in buyer behavior or market activity.

NY Dirt: Latest pied-à-terre update like “a night at improv”

The Real Deal — New York — Sat, 16 May 2026 11:30:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

New York Governor Kathy Hochul has proposed a two-step plan to implement a pied-à-terre tax on second homes, primarily targeting condos and co-ops with a "market value" of at least $1 million, which the Department of Finance estimates to be equivalent to a $5 million sales value. The second step, to be enacted two years later, involves creating an entirely new metric for valuing co-op and condo sales. Critics argue that the proposed valuation methods are unclear and inconsistent with actual sales prices, highlighting the complexity of implementing such a tax. The plan is seen by some as a deferral of a clear policy, raising doubts about the city's ability to develop a robust valuation system within the two-year timeframe.
