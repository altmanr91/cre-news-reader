Walker & Dunlop Investment Partners Outlines 2026 CRE Risks

Commercial Observer — Thu, 07 May 2026 20:02:51 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

Walker & Dunlop Investment Partners' experts recently discussed the significant risks facing the commercial real estate financial system, particularly within the multifamily sector. They highlighted that multifamily investments from 2019-2022 have largely underperformed due to a shift from low interest rates and cheap capital to today's higher rates, increased debt costs, and muted rent growth. This environment has created a demand for bridge financing as many construction loans mature on properties that are not yet stabilized or meeting original underwriting projections. The panel also emphasized the resilience of real estate-secured private credit compared to corporate private credit, noting that hard assets provide intrinsic value and foreclosure options for lenders. Furthermore, they pointed out a concerning trend of low distributions on paid investment (DPI) for real estate equity funds, impacting investor liquidity and future capital allocation decisions by institutional investors.

Digging Beneath the Confusion: The Future of Artificial Intelligence and Commercial Real Estate

Connect CRE — Fri, 08 May 2026 05:01:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

A new white paper from Cushman & Wakefield, "AI Impact on CRE: The Next 10 Years," argues that the future impact of artificial intelligence on commercial real estate will be less about technical capabilities and more about productivity gains related to hiring, revenue growth, and capital allocation. The report suggests that AI will not affect all real estate sectors equally, but rather will widen the distribution of outcomes across markets, property types, asset quality, and investment strategies, amplifying both positive and negative results. Key findings indicate that productivity gains may precede revenue growth and hiring, potentially delaying CRE demand, particularly in office markets. The United States is expected to lead in AI-driven impacts, while Europe and Asia-Pacific may experience slower adoption due to differing labor markets and policies. The report concludes that AI will introduce greater uncertainty and complexity, requiring occupiers to prioritize flexibility and high-quality environments, and investors to focus on asset selection and timing.

The U.S. industrial real estate market experienced robust absorption in Q1, narrowing the supply-demand gap for the first time in years, according to multiple market reports. Key trends include a flight to quality, with demand for modern, automation-ready facilities, and a resurgence in large-block leasing driven by 3PL providers and larger users. MARKET INTELLIGENCE

New construction remains largely concentrated in build-to-suit projects and those with tenant pre-commitments. Approximately 73% of deliveries in Q1 were speculative, an increase from 71% in 2025. Completions are expected to remain modest through 2026, but renewed groundbreakings in markets with healthy demand lifted the national development pipeline for a third straight quarter.

Opinion / Market Commentary

Tom Hallock, head of construction lending at Kiavi, offers insights into the current housing market, describing it as the most stable he has seen despite volatility. He notes that an adequate supply of housing is emerging, with some markets returning to 2019 levels, and buyers are gaining more choices. Hallock anticipates that the remainder of 2026 will bring more affordable options, with interest rates stabilizing in the 6% range, which he views as healthy for the market. He also discusses the build-to-rent (BTR) market, suggesting that proposed federal legislation to force investors to sell BTR properties after seven years is likely political noise that will lead to more sensible compromises, such as exemptions for communities of five or more contiguous units. Hallock believes the BTR model, particularly horizontal multifamily, will continue to thrive as the economic gap between renting and owning narrows, appealing to demographics like single women and young families.
