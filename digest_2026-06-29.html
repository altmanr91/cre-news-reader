Freddie Mac Underwriting Tightening in 2026

Commercial Observer — Mon, 29 Jun 2026 13:01:37 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

Freddie Mac's multifamily securitizations in early 2026 show a decisive tightening in underwriting, characterized by a weighted-average debt service coverage ratio (DSCR) of 1.41x and a 63.9% loan-to-value (LTV). This coverage is largely manufactured through interest-only (IO) structures, with approximately 95% of the balance featuring full-term or partial IO, allowing leverage to remain consistent with historical discipline while pricing adjusts. The floating-rate pools, particularly KF172, represent the highest concentration of risk due to thinner DSCRs and higher LTVs, making them vulnerable to sustained elevated SOFR rates. Acquisition activity is showing a healthy return, comprising about 40% of K series balance, indicating that transaction volume is recovering as borrowers adapt to higher costs of capital.

Even as retail spending hits new heights, consumers are more selective with what they’re buying

REJournals — Mon, 29 Jun 2026 09:51:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Market Research / Retail Report

Colliers' May U.S. Retail Foot Traffic & Sales Analysis indicates that while overall retail sales increased by 5.2% year-over-year in May, much of this growth was driven by inflation, particularly in gas station sales. Consumers are becoming more selective, prioritizing value-oriented purchases, apparel, and experiences like theaters and music venues, which saw significant foot traffic increases. Conversely, spending on larger discretionary items like furniture and home improvement, as well as visits to fast-food restaurants and banks, saw more modest gains or declines, reflecting increasing financial pressure on households.

America’s biggest housing push in decades sits in political limbo

The Real Deal — National — Sun, 28 Jun 2026 13:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

The article discusses the bipartisan 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act, a federal legislative effort aimed at addressing the national housing shortage and affordability crisis. The bill seeks to streamline environmental reviews, incentivize local housing production, expand manufactured housing definitions, and support accessory dwelling units. While the legislation faces political delays, its potential impact includes gradually increasing housing inventory and providing greater certainty for builders, developers, and investors by removing a controversial forced-sale provision for build-to-rent investors. The industry broadly supports the bill as a crucial step toward increasing housing supply, despite acknowledging that its effects will be gradual and local obstacles will persist.

California’s transfer tax fight just got upended. And Bay Area sellers want to be paid in AI stock

The Real Deal — San Francisco — Sun, 28 Jun 2026 15:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

California recently experienced significant legislative activity regarding real estate transfer taxes, with a citizen-led initiative to cap taxes at 0.11% qualifying for the November ballot. Assemblymember Buffy Wicks introduced AB 736, proposing caps of 1.5% for most jurisdictions and 3% for cities like San Francisco and Culver City, aiming to address developer concerns about high taxes hindering commercial-to-housing conversions. However, the citizen initiative was withdrawn after a deal with state lawmakers, leaving the future of Wicks' bill uncertain. Meanwhile, the Bay Area housing market is experiencing a frenzy driven by the AI industry boom, with some luxury home sellers attempting to trade properties for pre-IPO stock in AI companies like Anthropic and OpenAI, reflecting a broader scramble to capitalize on the AI gold rush.
