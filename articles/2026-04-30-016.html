Downtown Denver’s record vacancy reveals a geographic split

National - The Real Deal — Thu, 30 Apr 2026 21:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Market Research / Office Report

Downtown Denver's office market is experiencing a significant geographic split, with the central business district in upper downtown facing record-high vacancies exceeding 40%, while lower downtown, revitalized by the Union Station redevelopment, shows a more resilient vacancy rate around 20%. This bifurcation is further exacerbated by a flight to quality, where Class A buildings maintain lower vacancy rates compared to Class B and C properties. The city is responding with a $570 million tax increment financing fund to support office-to-residential conversions and acquire distressed assets, attracting opportunistic investors who are betting on the market's long-term recovery through adaptive reuse.

MARKET INTELLIGENCE

Downtown Denver's office market is characterized by a significant geographic split, with upper downtown experiencing vacancy rates above 40% and lower downtown around 20%. There is also a clear divide between Class A buildings (29-32% vacancy) and Class B/C properties (above 39% vacancy). The city has established a $570 million tax increment financing district to fund office-to-residential conversions and acquire distressed assets. Recent transactions include Luzzatto Company acquiring 1.8 million square feet of office towers for approximately $4.80/SF with plans for 1,200 residential units, and Brookfield Properties selling two towers for $29 million each ($41/SF). CP Group and Time Equities Inc. purchased Denver Place for $47.5 million ($51/SF). In contrast, desirable submarkets like Cherry Creek North command rents of $100/SF.
