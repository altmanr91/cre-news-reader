Banks Fueled The Rise Of Private Credit. Now They're 'Competing Against Ourselves'

Bisnow News Feed — Wed, 27 May 2026 17:24:16 -0400

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

Banks are facing a dilemma in the commercial real estate lending market, as their previous strategy of financing private credit funds has led to increased competition. After regulatory scrutiny and balance sheet issues constrained banks from 2023, they provided capital to debt funds, inadvertently empowering these alternative lenders. Now, as banks re-enter the market with a stronger position due to offloaded problem loans and potential policy changes, they find themselves competing against the very funds they helped capitalize. This dynamic has led banks to focus on smaller deals, largely avoiding construction lending, and continuing to act as servicers for private lenders by providing credit lines.

DATA POINTS

Banks' share of nonagency loan closings (last year): 34%

Alternative lenders' share of CRE debt originations (Q1): 53%

Debt fund deal activity surge (Q1): 280%

Banks' market share (Q1): 22%

Committed credit lines by largest U.S. banks to private credit vehicles (Q4 2024): $95B

Utilized credit lines by private credit vehicles (Q4 2024): $56B

Revolving credit lines for BDCs: $49B

Revolving credit lines for debt funds: $30B

MARKET INTELLIGENCE

CBRE's Lending Momentum Index indicates that banks reclaimed the top spot for nonagency loan closings last year, capturing 34% of the market, though this was below historic norms. Alternative lenders have since retaken the lead, responsible for 53% of CRE debt originations in the first quarter, with debt fund deal activity surging 280%, while banks' market share fell to 22%. Committed credit lines by the largest U.S. banks to private credit vehicles increased 145% over five years, reaching $95B by Q4 2024, with $56B utilized. Most bank borrowing by private credit occurs through revolving credit lines, totaling $49B for BDCs and $30B for debt funds.
