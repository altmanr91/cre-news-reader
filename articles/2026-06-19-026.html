Gauging the Obama Center’s real estate ripple effects across the South Side

The Real Deal — Chicago — Fri, 19 Jun 2026 11:15:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

The Obama Presidential Center, a 19-acre development on Chicago's South Side, has seen its construction costs escalate to $850 million. While intended as an economic catalyst, the project has fueled real estate speculation, leading to a doubling of home prices in East Woodlawn between 2019 and 2025. This surge has attracted out-of-state investors to the South Shore multifamily market, driving up unit costs but also resulting in a wave of foreclosures and property mismanagement due to a disconnect from local renter incomes. The situation has prompted legislative efforts to protect tenant rights and preserve affordability.

DATA POINTS

East Woodlawn single-family and multifamily home price increase (2019-2025): Doubled to a median of $440,000

60637 ZIP code vacant lots (over 10 years): Dropped from 964 to 798

Average cost of lower-end multifamily units in South Shore (2019): Less than $60,000 per unit

Average cost of lower-end multifamily units in South Shore (2024): $73,000 per unit

Foreclosure lawsuits against South Shore buildings (2024): 20

Delinquent Chicago multifamily loans in CMBS market from South Shore: Nearly a quarter

SPONSOR PIPELINE

South Stony Island Avenue: 28-story, 250-room hotel proposed by Allison S. Davis

MARKET INTELLIGENCE

The Institute for Housing Studies at DePaul University reported that single-family and multifamily home prices in East Woodlawn doubled between 2019 and 2025, reaching a median of $440,000, significantly outpacing the rate of home price growth for Illinois. The Cook County Assessor’s Office data, cited by Crain’s, indicates that the inventory of vacant lots in the 60637 ZIP code decreased from 964 to 798 over ten years. DePaul University also found that the average cost of lower-end multifamily units in South Shore increased from less than $60,000 in 2019 to $73,000 per unit in 2024. The Real Deal reported that in 2024, lenders filed 20 foreclosure lawsuits against South Shore buildings with five or more units, the highest volume in a decade for any Chicago neighborhood, and nearly a quarter of all delinquent Chicago multifamily loans in the CMBS market originate from South Shore.
