To succeed in today’s Twin Cities office sector? Landlords must work hard

REJournals — Mon, 04 May 2026 10:10:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Market Research / Office Report

Newmark's Q1 2026 report on the Minneapolis-St. Paul office market indicates a challenging but potentially stabilizing environment. While overall vacancy slightly dipped to 19.9% and net absorption turned positive, much of this improvement is attributed to a shrinking inventory as obsolete office space is removed through conversions and demolitions. Leasing activity remains subdued, characterized by a "flight to quality" where tenants right-size their footprints into newer, amenity-rich buildings, creating a sharp divide between Class A and Class B properties. Landlords are responding by holding asking rents steady but increasing concessions, particularly in the struggling downtown cores where vacancy rates are significantly higher. Despite these challenges, the report highlights opportunities for opportunistic investors willing to acquire discounted assets and reposition them, supported by the region's diverse economy and educated workforce.

Q1 2026 Downtown St. Paul Office Vacancy: 37%

MARKET INTELLIGENCE

Overall office vacancy across the Twin Cities dipped to 19.9% in Q1 2026, down from 20.8% at the end of 2025. Office net absorption in the Minneapolis-St. Paul market turned positive, reaching nearly 50,000 square feet during the first quarter. Since 2024, more than 3.4 million square feet of office space has been taken out of the Twin Cities office inventory. In downtown Minneapolis, vacancy hovered just above 30% in Q1 2026, while St. Paul’s downtown office market posted a vacancy rate of about 37%. The region’s unemployment rate stood at 3.8% at the end of 2025.
