Where New York’s budget landed on real estate

The Real Deal — New York — Fri, 29 May 2026 10:30:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Infrastructure Investment / Government Policy

New York State has approved a $268.5 billion budget that includes significant real estate policy changes. The reforms to the State Environmental Quality Review Act (SEQRA) will exempt certain housing projects in New York City and other urban areas from lengthy environmental reviews, a move expected to accelerate development and reduce costs. Additionally, a new pied-à-terre tax on luxury second homes in New York City, effective July 1, aims to generate $500 million annually for the city's budget, though its administration is anticipated to be complex, particularly for co-op properties.

DATA POINTS

State Budget Amount: $268.5 billion

Pied-à-terre tax annual revenue estimate: $500 million

Pied-à-terre tax effective date: July 1

Pied-à-terre tax target properties: 10,000 second homes valued at $5 million+

SEQRA exemption for NYC projects (medium/high-density): Up to 500 units

SEQRA exemption for NYC projects (low-density): Up to 250 units

SEQRA exemption for other urban areas: Up to 300 units

SEQRA exemption for rural communities: Up to 100 units

SEQRA exemption for areas without zoning codes: Up to 20 units

Estimated cost added by pre-certification for 500-unit project: $41 million

Estimated cost per apartment added by pre-certification: $82,000

Pied-à-terre tax rate for condos/co-ops ($1M-$3M): 4% annual surcharge

Pied-à-terre tax rate for condos/co-ops ($3M-$5M): 5.25% annual levy

Pied-à-terre tax rate for condos/co-ops ($5M+): 6.5% fee
