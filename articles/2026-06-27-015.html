D.C.’s real estate industry braces for democratic socialist mayor

The Real Deal — National — Fri, 26 Jun 2026 22:25:09 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

Washington D.C.'s real estate industry is facing heightened uncertainty following the mayoral primary victory of democratic socialist Janeese Lewis George, whose platform and potential tax policies are causing concern among developers. The city's economy is already strained by office distress, federal job cuts, and a slowdown in new construction, with property values in the central business district falling significantly over the past five years. Developers worry that Lewis George's progressive agenda, including potential support for a wealth tax, could further deter institutional investors who have already been withdrawing from the market. While Lewis George has expressed a goal of building 72,000 new housing units, developers emphasize that achieving this will require investor confidence, which they fear her policies may undermine.

DATA POINTS

Property value decline in central business district (last 5 years): almost 50%

Federal job cuts in Greater D.C. area: 100,000 jobs

City deficit: $1.1 billion

Proposed wealth tax surcharge on passive income and capital gains: 3%

Income threshold for wealth tax (individual): $400,000 per year

Income threshold for wealth tax (joint filers): $500,000 per year

Estimated wealth tax revenue (first year): $200 million

Estimated wealth tax revenue (second year): $100 million

Job loss in Greater D.C. area (January 2025-January 2026): 104,000 jobs

Residential vacancy in Greater D.C. area (2024): 6%

Residential vacancy in Greater D.C. area (2026): 8%

New housing units target (next 5 years): 72,000 units

MARKET INTELLIGENCE

New construction in Washington D.C. is at a 15-year low. The Greater D.C. area experienced the most job losses of any metro area in the country between January 2025 and January 2026. Residential vacancy in the region increased from 6% in 2024 to 8% in 2026.
