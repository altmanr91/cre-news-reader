The Definition of ‘Jobs’ in the Data Center Economy Needs to Shift

Commercial Observer — Fri, 22 May 2026 13:00:48 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

The author argues that the data center industry needs to redefine how it measures and communicates its job creation impact to local communities. Instead of focusing on the limited number of permanent, on-site jobs, the industry should emphasize the broader ecosystem of employment it generates, including construction, infrastructure, maintenance, and specialized technical roles. The core argument is that data center projects should be evaluated not by a fixed job count, but by their ability to create durable workforce pipelines that connect local residents to long-term opportunities in the digital and energy economy. This shift requires binding workforce development partnerships with local educational institutions and a more standardized, measurable approach to assessing long-term employment outcomes, especially when projects receive public incentives.

NYC On The Verge Of A Construction Boom As Permit Filings Spike

Bisnow News Feed — Fri, 22 May 2026 13:50:32 -0400

SUMMARY

Market Research / Construction Permits

New building permit filings in New York City surged in Q1 2026, with developers seeking permits for 21.2M SF across 577 new projects, a 74% year-over-year increase. This spike is largely driven by multifamily proposals, accounting for nearly half of the projects and over 16,800 units, a 251% increase in units since 2008. The increase is a response to the city's housing crisis and Mayor Eric Adams' goal of 500,000 new housing units over the next decade. However, the Real Estate Board of New York (REBNY) notes that the majority of new developments are smaller, under 99 units, due to labor wage requirements associated with the 485-x tax incentive, which developers argue restricts larger-scale projects needed to address the crisis. While the rebound is significant, REBNY cautions it's not yet indicative of a sustained return to record highs, especially given the 'yo-yo' effect observed with the expiration of the 421-a tax incentive.
