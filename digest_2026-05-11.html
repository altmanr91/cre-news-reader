All that new supply? It’s suppressing apartment rent growth

REJournals — Mon, 11 May 2026 09:56:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Market Research / Multifamily Report

A new report indicates that U.S. multifamily rent growth remains sluggish due to a historic wave of new supply, particularly in Sun Belt and Mountain West markets. While national average advertised rents saw a modest $4 increase in April to $1,758, year-over-year growth was negative 0.2%, and year-to-date growth was only 0.4%. Gateway and Midwest markets, including New York City, San Francisco, Chicago, and Minneapolis-St. Paul, are outperforming, with the Twin Cities projected for strong year-end growth due to more restrained new supply. Despite economic headwinds and moderating demand, opportunities may exist for investors in distressed assets and for operators focusing on expense reductions.

MARKET INTELLIGENCE

National multifamily advertised rents increased by $4 in April 2026 to $1,758, marking the second consecutive month of growth after winter declines. However, national rents were down 0.2% year-over-year and up only 0.4% year-to-date through April, which is about one-third of the average growth pace seen between 2012 and 2019. High-supply markets like Austin (-4.3%), Denver (-3.6%), Tampa (-3.4%), and Phoenix (-2.7%) continued to show weak annual rent growth. In contrast, Gateway and Midwest markets like New York City (4.8%), San Francisco (4.1%), Chicago (3.3%), and Minneapolis-St. Paul (2.4%) outperformed. The national multifamily occupancy rate stood at 94.2% in March, down 0.5% year-over-year. Nearly 480,000 rental units are forecast to be delivered nationally in 2026, followed by about 450,000 units annually thereafter.

Data Centers Are Becoming Industrial’s Growth Engine

Bisnow News Feed — Sun, 10 May 2026 10:51:07 -0400

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

The artificial intelligence boom is significantly boosting the U.S. industrial real estate sector, extending beyond just data center development to manufacturing and logistics. Major tech and manufacturing firms are partnering to build new facilities, with Nvidia and Corning planning three manufacturing plants in North Carolina and Texas. This trend is validated by a 28% jump in manufacturing leasing activity in Q1 2026 compared to the previous year, with 27% of new manufacturing leases attributed to data center and tech supply chain companies. The demand is driven by the ongoing need for IT gear and critical systems, suggesting a stabilized long-term growth engine for industrial real estate rather than a temporary construction-related surge. Additionally, a growing network of logistics facilities is emerging to support the data center sector, with data center suppliers now accounting for 10% of new industrial leases for major logistics landlords like Prologis.

Baltimore Industrial Recalibration: Driven by New Supply and Selective Leasing

REBusinessOnline — Mon, 11 May 2026 11:47:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

Baltimore's industrial market is undergoing a recalibration rather than a contraction, characterized by rising vacancy and elevated supply, yet sustained demand for high-quality assets. The market is exhibiting a 'barbell dynamic,' with strong performance in bulk logistics facilities in premier submarkets like Baltimore County East and smaller infill properties under 100,000 square feet. This divergence indicates that demand is becoming more selective, with capital increasingly disciplined and performance tied to asset quality and strategic positioning within the region's evolving logistics network. Despite broader market headwinds, institutional capital continues to invest, as evidenced by a significant portfolio acquisition, underscoring confidence in Baltimore's long-term strategic advantages as an East Coast logistics hub.

MARKET INTELLIGENCE

CoStar Group characterizes the Baltimore industrial market as undergoing a 'sharp correction' due to rising vacancy, elevated supply, and slower leasing activity. The market's vacancy rate is approximately 9.7%, with 3.2 million square feet of new deliveries over the past 12 months and negative trailing absorption of 2.4 million square feet. The new development pipeline stands at 2.1 million square feet. Baltimore County East leads the region with approximately 660,000 square feet of positive absorption over the trailing 12 months, holds 16.2% of market inventory, and has the most active construction pipeline at 664,000 square feet. Industrial transaction volume is holding near $1.1 billion, with pricing averaging approximately $142 per square foot and cap rates around 7.7%.

Despite Lincoln Park retail boom, LLJ working through $10M loan default

The Real Deal — Chicago — Sun, 10 May 2026 15:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

REO / Foreclosure

LLJ Ventures, a San Diego-based property investment firm, defaulted on a nearly $10 million loan against The Armitage Collection, a 33,000-square-foot retail property in Chicago's Lincoln Park. The loan, originated in late 2015, matured on January 6, leading special servicer Midland Loan Services to prepare for foreclosure and receivership. However, Midland is also considering a loan modification, and LLJ Ventures is reportedly close to an agreement to avoid formal foreclosure. The property is currently being marketed for sale by Mid-America Real Estate for $10.5 million, despite a recent appraisal valuing it at $13.1 million.

Michael Shvo, Germans’ exit from Transamerica Pyramid gets more complicated

SALE

The Real Deal — San Francisco — Sun, 10 May 2026 16:00:00 +0000

SUMMARY

The sale of San Francisco's iconic Transamerica Pyramid has become embroiled in a legal dispute, with a new court filing raising questions about the true compensation received by asset manager Michael Shvo. The property, along with two adjacent buildings, was sold by a partnership including German institutional investors and Shvo to Cyprus-based Yoda PLC for $692 million. While Shvo initially reported receiving $34 million in fees, a lawsuit by Deutsche Finance America alleges he received an additional $45 million upon termination as asset manager, bringing his total to $79 million, while Deutsche Finance America claims it was not paid its owed $31 million. This controversy unfolds as the investors incurred significant losses, having acquired the property for $650 million in 2020 and invested $250 million in renovations.
