Housing Notes: Removing Florida property taxes will reduce housing affordability

The Real Deal — National — Mon, 01 Jun 2026 21:30:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

Jonathan Miller, a prominent residential real estate analyst, argues that Florida Governor DeSantis' proposal to eliminate property taxes for primary residences, while seemingly beneficial, would ultimately reduce housing affordability. Miller contends that the plan, which would exempt the first $250,000 (and eventually $500,000) of a homesteaded home's value, primarily benefits older, long-tenured homeowners and shifts the tax burden to other revenue sources, such as sales taxes or new creative taxes. He highlights that the proposal would strip local governments of a significant portion of their controllable revenue, potentially leading to reduced services or increased taxes elsewhere. Furthermore, Miller suggests that the enhanced exemption, by disincentivizing home turnover, could exacerbate the already rapid home price appreciation in Florida, making homeownership less attainable for new buyers and first-timers.

Self-Storage Holds the Line: Why Chicago’s Quietest Asset Class Keeps Drawing Capital

REJournals — Tue, 02 Jun 2026 10:03:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

Self-storage in Chicago continues to attract significant capital due to its exceptional stability and low delinquency rates, which are significantly lower than the broader commercial mortgage-backed securities market. The sector benefits from natural supply constraints in Cook County, driven by high property taxes and a challenging development environment, which has prevented overbuilding seen in other markets. This disciplined supply, coupled with declining vacancy and strong rental rate increases, makes Chicago self-storage an attractive investment, with institutional and private capital remaining active despite a more selective lending environment. While cap rates have repriced from peak levels, they remain aggressive compared to other property types, and an upcoming maturity wall is expected to drive increased transaction activity, primarily for operationally challenged but not distressed assets.

DATA POINTS

Self-storage CMBS delinquency rate: 0.1%

Broader CMBS delinquency rate: 6%

Class A self-storage cap rates (Chicago): 4.9% to 5.7%

Class B self-storage cap rates (Chicago): 5.5% to 6.2%

MARKET INTELLIGENCE

Cook County's high property taxes and challenging development environment act as a natural constraint on new self-storage supply, limiting overbuilding compared to Sunbelt markets. Development activity is slowing across Chicagoland, and vacancy is declining. Lenders are more disciplined, focusing on in-place performance, stabilization assumptions, and sponsorship. The transaction pipeline is being shaped by loan maturities, with lenders increasingly focused on resolving loans and cleaning up balance sheets. Reassessment risk and expense growth assumptions can significantly impact self-storage valuations in Chicago.
