Flyover No More: The Case for Midwest Hotel Investment

REJournals — Thu, 07 May 2026 10:04:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

This article argues that the Midwest presents a compelling, often overlooked, investment opportunity for hospitality properties, contrasting it with the traditionally favored Sun Belt. The author highlights several advantages, including a balanced demand across cycles, lower supply risk due to limited new hotel construction, and significantly reduced insurance exposure compared to coastal markets. Key demand drivers in the Midwest include diversified economies in cities like Madison, expanding athletic calendars in Big Ten university towns, and robust medical travel anchored by major university hospital systems. While acknowledging seasonality as a downside, the article suggests that strategic investments in these demand-rich areas offer a long-term hedge for investors seeking stability and assets priced below replacement cost.

Insurance Drags Down Property Values By 17% In Climate-Sensitive Markets, Study Shows

Bisnow News Feed — Wed, 06 May 2026 18:19:21 -0400

SUMMARY

Market Research / Climate Risk

A new report by climate risk data firm First Street reveals that commercial real estate property values in climate-sensitive markets are 16.9% lower than in markets with lower climate risk. This valuation gap is primarily driven by the escalating and volatile costs of commercial property insurance, which saw a 154% increase between 2017 and 2024 across all major asset classes. Multifamily properties are particularly affected, experiencing a 207.5% rise in insurance costs per unit during the same period, leading to a 25% discount in trading values for high-risk multifamily assets. The study, based on 25 years of NCREIF data across 120 U.S. metro areas, highlights how increased insurance premiums are eroding net operating income, with insurance consuming 6.6% of multifamily NOI and 4.1% of all properties' NOI in 2024. While the reinsurance market is expected to soften, the underlying baseline for insurance rates continues to reset higher after each hardening period, indicating a persistent challenge for property owners in vulnerable areas.

MARKET INTELLIGENCE

Commercial real estate insurance costs grew by 154% between 2017 and 2024, with a compound annual growth rate of 14.3%. In contrast, the annual increase was 4.6% on average in the first 17 years of the 21st century. Multifamily insurance costs rose from $285.83 per unit in 2017 to $878.91 per unit in 2024, a 207.5% increase. Multifamily assets in high-risk markets have 69% higher premiums on average than those in low-risk areas, leading to a 25% discount in trading values. Insurance consumed 6.6% of multifamily NOI and 4.1% of all properties' NOI in 2024, up from 1.9% in 2017. Office values in Fort Lauderdale fell 17% between 2020 and 2024 as insurance costs doubled. Miami offices traded at an average of $360 per SF last year, nearly twice the national average of $190 per SF, and 38.5% above their valuations in Q1 2020.

SUMMARY

Opinion / Market Commentary

New York State Senator Patricia Faye has introduced a bill to implement a pied-à-terre tax on second homes in upstate municipalities, targeting properties valued between $2.5 million and $5 million. This threshold is notably lower than Governor Kathy Hochul's proposed $5 million minimum for New York City. The tax rates would range from 0.5% to 4%, with revenue split between local municipalities and the state's Aid and Incentives for Municipalities program. The initiative aims to redirect funds from vacation areas like the Hamptons and Saratoga Springs to surrounding communities in need. However, the proposal faces opposition from real estate professionals and some policy experts who argue it could deter investment and that the upstate second home market may not generate significant revenue.

Opinion / Market Commentary

A top housing official in the Mamdani administration, Leila Bozorg, acknowledged that New York City's Rent Guidelines Board (RGB) uses a "very blunt tool" by applying uniform rent increases across the diverse rent-stabilized housing stock. While not directly endorsing differentiated rent increases based on building characteristics like age, Bozorg indicated a need for a more holistic approach, including city investment in programs like an insurance initiative for affordable and rent-stabilized buildings, to support owners in covering rising costs. Vacancy rates still too high in Indianapolis office market. But is stability on the horizon?

REJournals — Thu, 07 May 2026 10:04:00 +0000

SUMMARY

Market Research / Office Report

The Indianapolis office market is showing signs of stabilization despite high vacancy rates, according to Colliers' Q1 2026 report. While the overall vacancy rate held steady at 21.2%, the downtown area continues to struggle with a record-high 27.7% vacancy. In contrast, suburban submarkets, particularly those north of the city like Carmel, Fishers, and Keystone Crossing, are experiencing stronger demand for Class-A space, with asking rents in some areas now surpassing the CBD due to a flight-to-quality trend. Leasing activity has increased, with 25,600 square feet of positive net absorption in Q1 and over 2.4 million square feet leased in the past year, a 24% year-over-year increase. Companies are generally reducing their footprints by 25-30% compared to pre-pandemic levels, and over 500,000 square feet of office space has been removed from inventory through conversions, with another 400,000 square feet expected to follow, helping to mitigate rising vacancies.

Asking rents for top-tier buildings in some suburban submarkets now exceed those in the CBD. Sublease availability has fallen from its peak in 2023. New construction is largely concentrated in suburban, mixed-use environments. Investors are targeting value-add opportunities, particularly in the Northwest submarket, acquiring underperforming assets at reduced prices for repositioning.
